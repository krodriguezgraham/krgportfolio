The Story

Started in print. Ended up designing life‑sustaining machines.

My design career started in graphic and print production — typography, color theory, and the discipline of working within rigid constraints. That foundation shaped everything that came after. When I moved into UX, I brought the same precision I'd applied to printed materials: every element earns its space, every decision has a reason.

Over six years I've moved across domains — graphic design, EdTech, e-commerce, consumer apps, and now medical devices — each one adding a new layer of complexity and a new set of stakeholders to navigate. Today I work as a UX Designer III at Fresenius Medical Care in Lawrence, MA, designing Class II and III medical devices for global clinical teams. I sit on a Human Factors & Usability Engineering team, which means I'm equally comfortable in a usability testing session and an FDA design review.

The thread running through all of it: I build bridges. Between design and engineering. Between clinical experts and product managers. Between what users need and what's technically feasible. That cross-functional fluency — developed across regulated industries and consumer products — is the most valuable thing I bring to a team.

Design Philosophy

Constraints aren't the enemy. Complacency is.

I've designed in FDA-regulated medical device environments where a wrong interaction pattern isn't a UX flaw — it's a patient safety event. That context permanently changed how I think about design. Rigor, in my experience, is not the opposite of creativity. It's the precondition for it.

Whether I'm designing a dialysis system for home patients or a consumer-facing digital product, the question I ask is the same: does this work for everyone who needs it, under the worst conditions they might face? That's not a medical device mindset. That's just good design.

01

Constraints are a brief

Regulations, timelines, tech debt — these aren't excuses. They're the actual design problem. I treat every constraint as a creative prompt.

02

Accessibility is a baseline

WCAG compliance from wireframe zero. Inclusive design patterns aren't retrofitted — they're foundational, and they make everything better for everyone.

03

Research before pixels

I don't wireframe assumptions. Contextual inquiry, stakeholder interviews, and competitive audits precede every design decision. The research is the design.

04

Systems over screens

A great screen in a broken system is still broken. I design with the ecosystem in mind — components, patterns, and handoff specs that scale across teams and time.
