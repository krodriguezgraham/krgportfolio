Executive Summary

Replacing a legacy batch system with an intelligent, ecosystem-native device.

This project designed the human-machine interface for an on-demand dialysate generator intended to replace the legacy batch dialysate system with a critical production cap of 60 liters per cycle. The legacy system created real patient safety risk: mid-treatment fluid shortfalls could interrupt care, and its alarm cascade was fully undifferentiated.

The design challenge wasn't simply replacing one UI with another. The new device would operate alongside the compact dialysis cycler with guided software — a modern treatment device with established user mental models — creating a core design tension between visual cohesion and functional differentiation. The solution required designing for an ecosystem, not just a screen.

"The goal wasn't to design a new device. It was to design a device that felt like it had always belonged next to the one they already trusted."

Problem Definition

Four compounding failure modes in a high-stakes clinical environment

The legacy batch dialysate system prepared fluid using a disposable cartridge producing up to 60 liters per cycle — enough for approximately three treatments. Under scrutiny, four distinct failure modes emerged that directly impacted both patient safety and clinical workflow efficiency.

Problem 01

60L Production Cap

If a patient required more fluid mid-treatment — due to high fluid gains or extended treatment needs — the machine could not produce it. The result: interrupted treatments, manual intervention, and delayed care.

Problem 02

Undifferentiated Alarm Cascade

All alarms used identical visual and auditory treatment regardless of urgency. Low-priority supply issues sounded identical to critical patient safety events, contributing directly to alarm fatigue among clinical staff.

Problem 03

Fragmented Ecosystem

The legacy device had no design relationship to the primary treatment device. Users were forced to manage two separate mental models, increasing cognitive load and extending training requirements.

The core design tension: the new device needed to feel like a native extension of the existing ecosystem — reducing the learning curve to zero — while behaving differently enough that clinicians could never confuse a supply alarm with a patient safety emergency.

Research & Discovery

Usability-led research with 15 participants across multiple rounds of testing

Working alongside Human Factors and Systems Engineers, I led a usability-centered research program with 15 participants: 10 in-center nephrology nurses and 5 home patients. Moderated usability testing was the primary method throughout — run iteratively from early prototypes through summative validation — supplemented by contextual inquiry and interviews to capture workflow context and mental models.

Usability Testing — Primary Method

The backbone of the research program. Moderated usability sessions with both in-center nurses and home patients using representative prototypes — measuring task success, error patterns, and alarm identification accuracy across iterative design rounds. Every major design decision traces directly to findings from these sessions.

Contextual Inquiry

Observed baseline workflows with in-center nurses to understand how clinical staff physically interacted with equipment during active dialysis therapy.

Semi-Structured Interviews

Gathered qualitative data on alarm handling behaviors, navigation patterns, and the mental models users had developed from extended use of the primary treatment device.

Key Insights

"Everything is an alarm. Low fluid sounds the same as a critical patient issue. After a while, you start tuning out."

— Nephrology Nurse, 15 years experience

"The cycler guides me through everything. I wish this device had that same feel — I already know how to use that."

— Home Patient, 6 years on dialysis

These two insights defined the entire design strategy. The alarm hierarchy had to map to actual risk levels to prevent fatigue. And the interface had to leverage the mental models users had already built with the primary treatment device — not ask them to learn from scratch.

The design opportunity: create a system with intuitive information architecture that reduces workflow friction, differentiates alarms by physiological risk to prevent fatigue, and leverages existing guided software mental models so users feel instant competence.

Design Principles

Four principles derived directly from what users told us

Each principle traced directly to a specific research finding. They weren't imposed top-down — they emerged from what clinical staff and home patients needed.

Principle 01

Familiar Foundation

Leverage the compact dialysis cycler's established visual language for all non-critical elements. Users transfer knowledge immediately, with zero learning curve for routine interactions.

Principle 02

Risk-Based Alarming

Alarm behavior — sound, color, frequency — must map to physiological risk level. Not all problems are equal. Undifferentiated alerts erode the clinical trust that makes alarms functional.

Principle 03

Interaction Efficiency

Reduce workflow friction for frequent tasks. In a clinical environment, every unnecessary step is cognitive overhead — and cognitive load is already a safety variable.

Principle 04

Guided Confidence

Provide step-by-step illustrated instructions whenever a user must intervene. Mirror the guided instruction philosophy: users should always know exactly what to do and why.

The Differentiated Layer: Risk-Based Alarm Hierarchy

The core innovation was a three-tier alarm hierarchy aligned with IEC 60601-1-8, the international standard for medical alarm systems. The hierarchy used both visual and auditory differentiation — nurses needed to identify not just urgency, but which device needed attention, often without looking up.

High Risk — Dialysis Cycler (Treatment Device)

Immediate Patient Safety

Example: air in bloodline. Red screen, flashing. Loud, urgent tone. Response required immediately. The generator never uses red for supply issues — red means the patient, always.

Medium Risk — Generator (Supply Device)

Treatment Disruption Risk

Example: concentrate empty. Yellow banner, flashing. Distinct lower-frequency tone (different from the treatment device). Intervention required within 5–30 minutes. Nurses hear the pitch and know: this is the fluid machine, not the patient.

Low Risk — Generator (Supply Device)

Informational

Example: prime complete. Green banner, status icon. Gentle chime. No action required. Confirms the system is working as expected without creating urgency.

Key Screens

Screen 01

Guidance Screen — Guided Instruction System Applied

When a user needs to initiate treatment or perform a critical task, they encounter a screen built directly on the guided instruction philosophy: simple step-by-step illustrations with numbered steps and a prominent "Guide Me" button they're already trained to press from their existing device.

  • Users transfer existing mental models — no new behaviors required.
  • Screen size note: the 5” generator display vs. the 8” treatment device display produces visible text size differences. This was raised as a design concern; change was confirmed out of scope for Phase 1 and documented for the roadmap.

Screen 02

Alarm State — Medium Acuity

When a supply event occurs — such as a supply compartment door being opened — the user sees a yellow banner at the top of screen, a distinct lower-frequency tone, and the affected area highlighted directly in the UI. A direct link takes them to the resolution screen with no additional navigation required.

  • The nurse knows by sound alone: this is the generator, not the patient.
  • Yellow signals "important, not life-threatening" — no ambiguity.
  • Action steps appear inline. No hunting, no menus.

Design Challenges

The hardest cross-functional negotiation

The Alarm Differentiation Pushback

Engineering initially preferred reusing existing alarm codes to reduce development time — a reasonable constraint. The problem: reusing codes would collapse the risk-based hierarchy we'd validated with users. By partnering with Human Factors and Systems Engineers and bringing user research data directly into the conversation, we reached a workable compromise: revise the existing alarm framework and reprioritize based on clinical risk rather than rebuilding from scratch.

The lesson: cross-functional collaboration requires bringing data — not just preference — to the table. User research gave us the standing to negotiate, not just advocate.

Retrospective

What worked, what I'd do differently

What Worked Well

01

Research-First Foundation

The alarm fatigue insight and the systematic analysis of where users got stuck shaped the entire design strategy. Without that foundation built on real user behaviour, we would have solved the wrong problems.

02

Visual Language Mapping

Systematic analysis of the treatment device ensured ecosystem consistency without guesswork. Users felt instant competence with the new device because the design work had been done upstream — before anyone saw a prototype.

03

Risk-Based Differentiation

This wasn't about making things look different — it was about making them functionally different in ways that map to real clinical stakes. The multi-sensory alarm approach addressed a genuine patient safety gap while preserving ecosystem cohesion.

What I'd Do Differently

01

Earlier Engineering Engagement

Closer engineering involvement from the start would have surfaced processing constraints sooner, preventing early design assumptions about features that ultimately required deferral to Phase 2.

02

Earlier Home Patient Usability Testing

Home patients had distinct interaction needs that surfaced later in the process than ideal. Running dedicated usability sessions with home patients earlier — not just as part of combined cohorts — would have informed the IA and alarm design from the beginning rather than prompting adaptation mid-project.

03

Longitudinal Usability Testing

Four months of testing captured initial reactions but not long-term adoption patterns. A 6–12 month follow-up study would validate whether alarm fatigue reduction holds over time and through staff turnover — data that's clinically meaningful and commercially valuable.

Why This Work Matters to Me

Designing for people who don't get to opt out.

Most of the people who use the devices I design didn't choose to need them. They're managing a chronic condition that requires sitting connected to a machine for hours at a time, multiple times a week — at a clinic, or at home, often alone. That reality sits with me throughout every design decision. When I'm debating whether an alarm pattern is clear enough, or whether a guidance screen has one step too many, I'm thinking about a home patient at 6am trying to figure out what the machine is telling them before they've had coffee. I'm thinking about a nurse managing four patients simultaneously who can't afford to read every alert at length.

That's not a constraint. That's the design brief. And it's the kind of brief that sharpens you.

The most meaningful feedback I received wasn't a metric. It was a nurse in usability testing saying: "The yellow caution means I have time. The red alarm means drop everything." That distinction — built into the design intentionally — is the difference between a tool that works and one that erodes trust over time.

What I've come to understand about myself as a designer is that I'm drawn to complexity that has real stakes. Medical devices sit at the intersection of regulatory rigor, engineering constraints, clinical workflow, and deeply human need — and navigating all of those simultaneously is what I find most engaging. Not despite the constraints, but because of them. Constraints are where the real design work happens.

I'm also drawn to the parts of a project that outlast it. The alarm hierarchy framework and design system built on this project are now being applied across three future products at Fresenius Medical Care. That kind of leverage — where decisions made thoughtfully on one product shape how a whole family of products behaves — is what senior design work looks like to me. It's not just solving the problem in front of you. It's building the foundation that makes the next problem easier to solve well.

This project asked me to hold a lot at once: patient safety, ecosystem coherence, engineering feasibility, regulatory compliance, and the simple human fact that the people using this device were already carrying a heavy load. I'm proud of how the team navigated that. And I'm proud that the work we did is still in use — still protecting patients, still training nurses — long after the project closed.
