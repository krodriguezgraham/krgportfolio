Unified Online Learning Platform — Remote Education, Simplified

A desktop platform designed to consolidate the fragmented tools of remote education into a single integrated workspace for students and educators. Completed as part of a UX Design Fellowship.

Learning Project: Completed during a UX Design Fellowship. This was a first project — included because process and growth are worth showing. The methods are foundational; the reflection is honest.

The Brief

COVID sent education online. The tools didn't go with it.

When COVID-19 pushed schools entirely remote, educators and students didn't get a unified system — they got a pile of disconnected tools. Video calls on one platform, assignments on another, grades somewhere else, and communication scattered across email, apps, and parent portals. At the time I was working as an educator, and I was living this problem every day.

The design brief was self-assigned: build something that could consolidate all of it into one place — intuitive enough that a student could navigate it independently, and comprehensive enough that an educator could run an entire school day from a single window.

"Having 23 tabs open while terrified of accidentally closing the wrong one isn't a workflow. It's anxiety with a browser."

Research

Educators as primary subjects — because I was one.

I interviewed educators across levels — elementary, middle, and high school, public and private — and distributed surveys on remote teaching technology. The insider perspective shaped both the questions I asked and my ability to interpret the answers with nuance. The finding was unanimous: the problem wasn't any single tool. It was the fragmentation.

Designing from lived experience is a double-edged advantage: you understand the problem deeply, but you have to work harder to separate your own assumptions from actual user needs. Structured interviews were essential to keeping the research honest.

Affinity mapping — interview insights clustered to identify design priorities

Competitive Analysis

Finding a genuine comparable was harder than expected. One desktop app — Shift — addressed the multi-account problem but didn't unify curriculum, grading, or communication meaningfully. That gap confirmed the opportunity.

Comparative analysis of Shift — the closest existing solution and where it fell short

Define & Ideate

Two users, one platform — neither can be an afterthought.

The design challenge was inherently dual-sided: a platform that worked for educators managing lesson delivery, grading, and attendance — and simultaneously worked for students navigating assignments, deadlines, and communication. Designing for one without deeply considering the other would break the whole system.

The Educator

Remote Teacher · Multi-Device · Overwhelmed

Core Frustrations

  • 23+ browser tabs open simultaneously
  • Three devices to manage a single school day
  • No single place for grades, attendance, and lessons

What They Need

  • One window for everything — grades, curriculum, calendar
  • Integrated student and parent communication
  • Maximum two devices to run a full day

The Student

High Schooler · Remote Learner · Disorganized

Core Frustrations

  • Important emails buried and missed
  • No central place for assignments and deadlines
  • Disconnected tools with no unified calendar

What They Need

  • One place for class materials, assignments, and due dates
  • Easy, direct teacher contact
  • Calendar that syncs and notifies automatically

Sketching & Wireframing

Desktop-first, Google Suite as the integration backbone.

Initial sketches centered the three pillars educators had named most urgently: communication, grading, and curriculum. Low-fidelity wireframes used Google Suite as the assumed integration layer — the most universally adopted toolset across all institutions interviewed — making the familiar feel native rather than bolted on.

Testing & Results

An honest test — and what the messy results actually taught me.

Usability testing was conducted on the medium-fidelity prototype with a mixed group of educators and non-educators. Participants completed two structured tasks: uploading homework as a student, and scheduling a meeting as an educator. The results were humbling — and instructive.

What the data actually meant

01

CTA affordance was the core issue. Buttons for primary actions — upload, submit, schedule — weren't visually prominent enough. Adjusting color contrast and increasing text weight on key actions was the direct fix.

02

Wrong participants skewed the findings. Non-educators gave generic usability feedback without engaging with the platform's actual purpose. This was a research design error — future studies would screen exclusively for the target user group.

03

Free exploration was a signal, not a failure. Users abandoning tasks to explore freely suggested the interface felt compelling. The wayfinding just needed to be stronger to channel that curiosity into task completion.

What I Learned

First-project lessons that still apply at the senior level.

01

Patience with ambiguous data is a design skill

When usability results came back mixed and hard to parse, the instinct was frustration. The real lesson: ambiguous data tells you the test design needs refinement just as much as the product does. I now treat unclear results as a prompt to improve the research method, not just the interface.

02

Test with your actual users — not convenient ones

Feedback from non-educators was almost entirely noise. Recruiting the right participants matters as much as writing the right test script. This shaped how I approach participant recruitment on every research study since.

03

Dual-user systems are systems problems

Designing a platform where the educator's output is the student's input introduced me to systems thinking before I had vocabulary for it. That same lens now drives how I approach design systems, cross-functional workflows, and any product with multiple stakeholder types.
