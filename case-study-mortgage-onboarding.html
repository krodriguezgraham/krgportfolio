Redesigning a Mortgage Pre-Qualification Flow to Earn Trust Before Asking for Data

Consumer Fintech UX Research Information Architecture Speculative Redesign WCAG 2.2 Mobile-First Figma
Type
Speculative Redesign
Domain
Consumer Mortgage / Fintech
Timeline
3 Weeks
Platform
Mobile-First Web
Role
Sole Designer & Researcher

Speculative Redesign: This project is not affiliated with any mortgage lender. It applies the same research-led methodology I use in regulated medical device work to a high-stakes consumer financial context — where trust, anxiety, and irreversible decisions create UX challenges that are structurally similar to clinical environments.

The Problem

A mortgage application is one of the most stressful experiences in a consumer's life. Most digital flows make it worse.

Buying a home is the largest financial decision most people will ever make. Pre-qualification — the first digital step in that process — should reduce anxiety and build confidence. Instead, most mortgage pre-qualification flows front-load data collection, bury explanations, use jargon without definition, and provide no sense of progress or safety. The result is a flow that feels adversarial rather than supportive.

I chose this problem space deliberately. The UX challenges in consumer fintech — high stakes, irreversible decisions, users operating under stress, regulatory compliance requirements — are structurally similar to the medical device environments I work in daily. The transfer is direct: the same design principles that prevent alarm fatigue in an ICU prevent abandonment in a mortgage flow.

The core problem: the flow asks for sensitive personal and financial data before explaining why it's needed or what happens to it. Trust is never established — it's just assumed.

Research & Discovery

Five interviews. One overwhelming theme: people feel interrogated, not guided.

I conducted five semi-structured interviews with first-time homebuyers who had completed a mortgage pre-qualification within the past 18 months — across three different lenders. I supplemented this with a heuristic audit of four major mortgage pre-qualification flows (Rocket Mortgage, Better.com, Chase, and loanDepot) evaluated against Nielsen's 10 heuristics and WCAG 2.2.

Finding 01

Data collection precedes trust-building

Every flow audited asked for SSN, income, and employment details before explaining what would happen with that data, what pre-qualification meant, or whether it would affect credit. Users described feeling "tricked into" submitting sensitive information.

Finding 02

Jargon without definition creates decision paralysis

Terms like "DTI ratio," "LTV," and "hard pull vs. soft pull" appeared without explanation in 3 of 4 flows audited. 4 of 5 interviewees reported stopping to Google a term mid-application — and two abandoned the flow entirely after doing so.

Finding 03

No sense of what comes next

None of the audited flows showed users how long the process would take, what the steps were, or what would happen after they submitted. Users described this as "flying blind" — amplifying anxiety at every screen transition.

Finding 04

Error states are punishing, not instructive

Validation errors across all four flows used generic messages ("Invalid input") with no guidance for correction. First-time buyers with non-standard income situations — freelancers, recently changed jobs — described repeated form failures as "humiliating."

"I didn't know if clicking 'Continue' would affect my credit score. So I just closed the tab."

This quote from a 31-year-old first-time buyer captures the core failure precisely. The drop-off wasn't caused by a lack of motivation — it was caused by a lack of safety. The user didn't trust the flow enough to take the next step. This is the same dynamic I work against in clinical environments: when users don't trust a system's behavior, they disengage — and in a medical context, disengagement costs health outcomes. In a mortgage context, it costs conversions and, more importantly, a person's path to homeownership.

Design Decisions

Three structural changes. Each one earned by the research.

The redesign didn't require adding features — it required reordering priorities. The data the lender needs is the same. What changes is the sequence, the framing, and the amount of context provided before asking for anything sensitive.

Decision 01 — Trust before data

Lead with a plain-language explanation of what pre-qualification is and isn't

The first screen now shows: what the process involves, that the initial check is a soft pull (no credit impact), how long it takes, and what the user will get at the end. No data collection until the user has confirmed they understand what they're entering. This mirrors a principle I apply in medical device design — never ask a user to act on something they haven't been given the context to understand.

  • "This is a soft credit check — it won't affect your credit score" appears before any PII is requested.
  • Step count ("3 of 7") and estimated time ("About 8 minutes") shown persistently in the header.
  • Plain-language definitions appear inline via expandable tooltips — no leaving the flow to Google.

Decision 02 — Progressive disclosure of sensitive data

Sequence data requests from low-stakes to high-stakes

The original flow asked for SSN on screen 3 of 12. In the redesign, SSN is requested on screen 9 — after the user has already invested time, received partial results, and been shown why the remaining data is needed. By the time sensitive data is requested, trust has been established through demonstrated competence and transparency.

  • Property goals and location collected first — low stakes, builds momentum.
  • Employment and income in the middle — moderate stakes, context provided for each field.
  • SSN and hard-pull consent collected last, with explicit explanation of purpose and a "why we need this" expandable section.

Decision 03 — Error states that teach, not punish

Validation errors become guidance, not dead ends

Each error state was redesigned to answer three questions: what went wrong, why it matters, and what to do next. For non-standard income situations — self-employed, recently changed jobs, multiple income streams — the error state now branches into an alternative collection path rather than failing outright.

  • Inline validation triggers on field exit, not on form submit — users catch errors before they've invested in completing the page.
  • Non-standard income types get a dedicated branch with tailored fields and explanatory copy.
  • Every error includes a direct path to chat support for edge cases the form can't handle.

Before & After

The same information architecture. A fundamentally different experience of it.

The data collected is identical. The lender's requirements haven't changed. What changed is the order, the framing, and the amount of context provided at each step — making the same journey feel guided rather than interrogated.

Before — original flow
  • No intro screen — opens directly into data entry
  • Credit impact unexplained until legal fine print at submit
  • SSN requested on screen 3 of 12
  • Jargon undefined ("DTI," "LTV," "hard pull")
  • No step count or time estimate
  • Generic error messages on validation failure
  • Non-standard income types: no path, hard fail
After — redesigned flow
  • Screen 1: plain-language explanation, soft-pull confirmation, time estimate
  • Credit impact stated explicitly on screen 1, before any data collected
  • SSN deferred to screen 9 of 12
  • All jargon defined inline via expandable tooltips
  • Persistent step count and time estimate in header
  • Error states: what went wrong, why, what to do next
  • Non-standard income: dedicated branch with tailored fields

Outcomes & Validation

Validated with the same people who abandoned the original.

I ran moderated usability testing on the redesigned prototype with 4 of the 5 original interview participants — giving each a task to complete a pre-qualification for a hypothetical home purchase. Feedback was evaluated against the four original research findings.

4 / 4 completed

All four participants completed the redesigned flow without abandoning — including the two who had abandoned during original research.

0 mid-flow Google searches

No participant left the flow to look up a term. Inline definitions were used 11 times across 4 sessions.

Trust — unprompted

3 of 4 participants used the word "transparent" without prompting when describing the redesigned flow. None used it to describe the original.

"This one actually tells me what it's doing and why. I don't feel like I'm signing something I don't understand."

Retrospective

What carries from regulated environments into consumer fintech — and what doesn't.

01

High-stakes consumer UX and regulated clinical UX share a root cause of failure

In both contexts, the core failure is the same: asking users to act without giving them the information they need to act safely. In a dialysis system, this means an alarm without a risk level. In a mortgage flow, it means a "Continue" button without a credit impact disclosure. The design solution is structurally identical — provide context before requesting action, not after.

02

Consumer users self-advocate in ways clinical users can't

A nurse in an ICU can't abandon a patient monitor mid-task. A homebuyer can close a browser tab instantly. This makes abandonment more abrupt and less recoverable in consumer contexts — which means the threshold for clarity and trust needs to be met earlier in the flow, not just eventually.

03

What I'd do differently with more time

A/B test the SSN deferral specifically — it's the highest-risk structural decision and the one most likely to have business pushback from a conversion team optimising for early data capture. I'd also want to validate the error-state branches with the self-employed cohort specifically, as that group's needs drove those design decisions and they weren't represented in the usability test.
